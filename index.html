Mental Health Issue: Anxiety

1. What is it?

Anxiety is more than just everyday worry. It becomes a disorder when it is excessive, persistent (lasting more than six months), and interferes with daily life.

There are several types of anxiety disorders, including:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
  • Panic Disorder
  • Specific Phobias
  • Social Anxiety Disorder
  • Separation Anxiety Disorder
  • Agoraphobia
  • Selective Mutism

Fear is a response to an immediate, identifiable threat, while anxiety is distress or worry without a clear or immediate danger.

2. Prevalence

  • Globally: Affects nearly 30% of adults during their lifetime, 4% at any given time.
  • Australia: Around 14.4% of Australians (16–85) experience an anxiety disorder in a 12-month period.
  • Young People: Up to 39% of Australians aged 16–24 report a mental disorder in the past year, with anxiety leading. Subclinical anxiety is rising.

3. Signs & Symptoms

Emotional & Cognitive Symptoms

  • Persistent, uncontrollable worry or fear
  • Restlessness, irritability, difficulty concentrating

Physical Symptoms

  • Racing heart (palpitations), muscle tension, headaches, stomach aches
  • Dizziness, sweating, tremors, shortness of breath, dry mouth

Behavioral Symptoms

  • Avoiding triggering situations (social settings, specific phobias)
  • Interference with daily life, school, work, or relationships

4. Risk Factors / Causes

Biological & Genetic Factors

  • Family history of anxiety or mental health conditions
  • Brain pathways that mismanage threat responses

Environmental & Psychological Factors

  • Childhood trauma, neglect, abuse, or loss
  • Ongoing stress (financial, academic, workplace pressures)
  • Adverse life events

Other Risk Factors

  • Women are more likely to be affected
  • Onset commonly occurs between 15–35 years (often before 25)
  • Poverty, chronic stress, or family history increase vulnerability

5. Treatments / Strategies

Therapies

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Effective for adults and youth, including online programs.
  • Exposure Therapy (ERP): Effective for phobias and OCD.

Medications

  • SSRIs & SNRIs: First-line treatment
  • Other: tricyclic antidepressants, benzodiazepines (short-term), beta-blockers

Lifestyle & Self-Help Strategies

  • Exercise, mindfulness, relaxation techniques
  • Journaling, sleep hygiene, social connection

Early Intervention

Helps prevent escalation of subclinical anxiety through routines, mindfulness, and social support.

6. Where to Go for Help?

  • General Practitioner (GP): First contact, can refer to psychologists under Medicare Better Access program.
  • Psychologists / Mental Health Professionals: Provide CBT and other therapies.
  • Online Resources: APS, Healthdirect, NIMH
  • Support Campaigns: “Can We Talk?” promotes awareness and coping strategies.
