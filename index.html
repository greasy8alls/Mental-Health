What is Anxiety?

Everyone feels anxious sometimes. It becomes an anxiety disorder when worry or fear is excessive, persistent (often for months) and starts to interfere with study, work, relationships or day‑to‑day life. Anxiety disorders include generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), social anxiety, panic disorder, specific phobias, separation anxiety, agoraphobia, and more.

Key idea: fear is a response to a clear danger (e.g., a snake), while anxiety is the body’s alarm system when the threat is uncertain or future‑focused.

Prevalence

Australia

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions. Around 1 in 7 Australians experience an anxiety disorder in a 12‑month period. Young people (16–24) report high rates compared with other age groups.

Worldwide

Globally, anxiety disorders are among the most prevalent mental health conditions, affecting a significant portion of people at some point in life.

Note: exact figures vary by report and year; always check the date and method used by the source.

Signs & Symptoms

Emotional / Thinking

  • Persistent worry, dread or fear that’s hard to control
  • Feeling on edge, irritable or overwhelmed
  • Difficulty concentrating; racing thoughts

Physical

  • Racing heart, shortness of breath
  • Muscle tension, headaches, stomach aches, nausea
  • Dizziness, sweating, shaking, trouble sleeping

Behaviour

  • Avoiding situations (e.g., social events, classes, travel)
  • Seeking excessive reassurance or safety behaviours
  • Impact on school, work, friendships or family life

Risk Factors & Causes

Biology & Genetics

  • Family history of anxiety or mood disorders
  • Differences in brain circuits that process threat and stress

Psychology & Environment

  • Stressful life events, trauma, grief or loss
  • Chronic stress, bullying, academic pressure
  • Health conditions, substances or medications that mimic anxiety
Remember: Anxiety is usually caused by a mix of factors—not one single thing.

Treatments & Strategies

Evidence‑based therapies

  • CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy) to change unhelpful thinking and behaviours
  • Exposure‑based strategies to gently face fears and reduce avoidance
  • In some cases, other therapies like ACT or mindfulness‑based approaches

Medications

  • First‑line: SSRIs or SNRIs (prescribed by a GP/psychiatrist)
  • Others may be used short‑term or for specific symptoms (clinical guidance required)

Self‑care & daily habits

  • Regular physical activity and sleep routine
  • Mindfulness, slow breathing, muscle relaxation
  • Healthy boundaries with screens/caffeine; balanced nutrition
  • Stay connected with supportive friends/family
Tip: Early help tends to lead to better outcomes. If strategies aren’t helping, speak with a professional.

Where to Get Help (Australia)

Start here

  • GP (family doctor): first step for assessment, referrals and care plans
  • Psychologist / Counsellor: ask about CBT or youth‑friendly services
  • School counsellor / wellbeing leader

Helplines & online support

  • Healthdirect
  • Australian Psychological Society
  • NIMH – Anxiety Disorders
  • Black Dog Institute
  • headspace (12–25 years)

Urgent? If you or someone else is in immediate danger, call 000 (Australia). For crisis support, call Lifeline 13 11 14 or chat online at their website.

Extension: Young People at Higher Risk

  • Experiencing grief or loss, trauma or major change
  • Living with disability or chronic illness
  • Family breakup/divorce or conflict at home
  • LGBTI+ young people due to minority stress and discrimination

Supportive environments, inclusive policies, and early, culturally safe care make a difference.
