Mental Health Issue: Anxiety

1. What is it?

Anxiety is more than normal worry. It becomes a disorder when it is excessive, persistent (lasting more than six months), and affects daily functioning. Anxiety disorders include:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
  • Panic Disorder
  • Specific Phobias
  • Social Anxiety Disorder
  • Separation Anxiety Disorder
  • Agoraphobia
  • Selective Mutism

Difference from fear: Fear is a reaction to a recognized threat, whereas anxiety occurs without an immediate danger, often causing distress and interference in life.

2. Prevalence

  • Global: ~30% of adults experience anxiety disorders at some point in life; 4% at any one time.
  • Australia: 14.4% of people (16–85) experienced an anxiety disorder in a 12-month period; most common mental health condition in the country.
  • Youth: 39% of Australians aged 16–24 reported a mental disorder in the past year, including anxiety. Subclinical anxiety is rising, causing sleep issues, muscle tension, and persistent worry.

3. Signs & Symptoms

Emotional & Cognitive

  • Persistent, uncontrollable worry or fear
  • Restlessness, irritability, difficulty concentrating
  • Racing thoughts, indecision, overthinking

Physical

  • Racing heart, palpitations
  • Muscle tension, headaches, stomach aches
  • Dizziness, tremors, sweating, dry mouth, shortness of breath

Behavioral

  • Avoidance of triggering situations
  • Reduced participation in work, school, or social life
  • Subclinical anxiety sufferers may maintain routines but still experience distress

4. Risk Factors / Causes

Biological & Genetic

  • Family history of anxiety or mental health disorders
  • Brain pathway dysfunction affecting threat response

Environmental & Psychological

  • Childhood trauma, abuse, neglect, or loss
  • Chronic stress in work, school, or home life
  • Life adversity combined with genetic predisposition

Other Factors

  • Women are more likely affected
  • Age of onset usually 15–35 years (often before 25)
  • Poverty, family history, and chronic stress increase risk

5. Treatments / Strategies

Therapies

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Proven effective for adults and youth, including online self-help programs
  • Exposure Therapy (ERP): Useful for phobias and OCD

Medications

  • SSRIs & SNRIs (first-line)
  • Other options: tricyclic antidepressants, benzodiazepines (short-term), beta-blockers

Lifestyle & Self-Help

  • Regular exercise, mindfulness, relaxation techniques
  • Journaling, sleep hygiene, social connection
  • Routine, nutrition, and early intervention for subclinical anxiety

6. Where to Go for Help?

  • General Practitioner (GP): First contact; can refer to psychologists or specialists
  • Psychologists / Mental Health Professionals: CBT and other therapies (available via Medicare Better Access in Australia)
  • Online Resources: APS, Healthdirect, NIMH (USA)
  • Support Campaigns: “Can We Talk?” encourages early help-seeking and coping strategies
